About the Long Tail

The long tail describes how the distribution of impact across maternity interventions is highly skewed: a small number of top interventions account for the majority of preventable infant deaths, while many further interventions each contribute modestly on their own.

The standalone view shows each intervention's impact in isolation — which single services matter most if deployed alone. The marginal view shows what each service adds once all others are already in place — often much smaller, because interventions overlap in who they benefit and interact multiplicatively.

The crucial insight is that the cumulative impact of the "tail" — many individually small interventions — can rival the impact of the top few. A strategy that funds only the top services by standalone impact may therefore miss significant gains from collectively funding the long tail.

Model Overview

This simulation model explores how multiple maternal health interventions, working together across a diverse population, might contribute to reducing infant mortality. It pays particular attention to how different combinations of services might impact different population groups, and considers a number of strategies for allocating scarce resources. The simulation models infant mortality across 10,000 births assigned to 40 intersectional groups (8 ethnic groups × 5 deprivation quintiles) drawn from a population resembling England's demographic makeup. 30 maternity interventions are simulated, and the impact multiple scenarios of service combinations on mortality risk across population groups are analysed.

This model is a simulation, not a forecast, prediction, or descriptive analysis, and the purpose is to demonstrate how the long tail of interventions might naturally appear, how this might affect commissioning or health systems decisions, and what the effects might be on groups experiencing health inequalities. All parameters are illustrative, and althought they are intended to be plausible they are not derived from systematic review. Its purpose is to make visible the structural relationships between intervention scale, population diversity, and health equity — particularly the cumulative importance of smaller, more targeted interventions that might be challenging to consider in conventional evaluation.

Every birth in the model is assigned 1) to population groups, 2) health services that they might benefit from, and 3) a risk of infant mortality. Health services that they might benefit from are influenced by population group (need), and in turn population group and need influence the risk of infant mortality. based on the health interventions they need and their intersectional group. A birth in a white British deprived group is more likely to need smoking cessation support; their risk of mortality is increased due to smoking during pregnancy, and some or all of this increased risk can be mitigated by receiving smoking cessation support. In a scenario where smoking cessation support is available, their risk of mortality is reduced.

Detailed Model Structure

The model is built from three linked tables.

Table One describes the 30 interventions. Each intervention is assigned a risk ratio representing its direct effect on infant mortality when received by someone who needs it — for example, a risk ratio of 0.55 means a 45% reduction in risk. The table also captures how interventions interact in pairs: 101 specific pairs are assigned interaction effects, representing cases where receiving both services together produces a benefit greater than either alone. This is important because some interventions have little direct effect but substantially enable others. Language translation, for instance, is modelled with no standalone mortality benefit, but it improves the effectiveness of clinical services that depend on communication and informed consent. Each intervention also carries a cost per recipient and a fixed overhead cost.

Table Two defines eight ethnic groups and five deprivation quintiles, each with a baseline infant mortality rate, a population share, and the probability that a group member will need each of the 30 services. These probabilities vary across groups in ways that reflect known patterns: sickle cell screening is relevant only for Black Caribbean and Black African groups, smoking cessation need is highest among White British individuals, and so on. Ten of the 30 interventions are group-specific; the remaining 20 are universal in principle, though need probabilities still differ between groups.

Table Three contains the 10,000 simulated individuals. Each person is randomly assigned an ethnic group and then a deprivation quintile, where the deprivation assignment depends on ethnicity reflecting the real correlation between the two in England. For each person, service needs are then drawn as random yes/no outcomes based on probabilities that combine their ethnic group needs with deprivation group needs and a random intersectional-specific weighting.

Intersectional groups: The combination of eight ethnic groups and five deprivation quintiles creates 40 intersectional categories. Each person's baseline mortality is shaped by three factors multiplied together: their ethnic group's rate, a deprivation rate (the most deprived quintile carries 1.6 times the reference rate; the least deprived, 0.7 times), and an intersectional factor for selected combinations where disadvantage compounds beyond what ethnicity and deprivation alone would predict. For example, Black African women in the most deprived quintile receive an additional 15% uplift to their baseline risk, while White British women in the least deprived quintile receive a 10% reduction.

The same logic applies to service need: specific intersectional groups may have elevated need for particular services beyond what their ethnic and deprivation profiles would separately suggest.

How risk is calculated: For each individual, the model generates an unadjusted risk which represents what their mortality risk would be with no interventions at all.

The unadjusted risk combines population group and the health needs of an individual. Each service needed increases the individual's baseline risk; if smoking cessation support is needed, that implies a person smoked during pregnancy and this increases risk on the pregnancy outcome by 1.5x greater than their baseline. Multiplying all need-risks together would produce impossibly high risks for people needing many services so the model applies dampened compounding: the multipliers are ranked from largest to smallest, and each successive multiplier contributes progressively less — the highest contributes its full effect, the second about 71%, the third about 58%, and so on so that additional health problems add diminishing independent risk.

When interventions are available, each person benefits from those services that they both need and receive. Services that they do not need have no impact on their risk of the outcome. The combined effect is the product of all relevant individual risk ratios and their pairwise interaction terms, so that the impact on overall outcomes depends on the complete basket of health services and is slightly non-linear. An individual's adjusted risk (applied in strategies in the model) is the unadjusted risk multiplied by the combined effect of the all the services they benefit from.

Each intervention has two cost components: a variable cost incurred per person who receives it, and a fixed overhead cost incurred once to make the service available regardless of how many people use it. The total cost of any combination of interventions is the sum of all individual-level variable costs plus the overhead for each included service. Cost-effectiveness is expressed as cost per death prevented — the total cost divided by the number of deaths averted compared to providing no services at all. Total cost of all 30 services is approximately £16.6M.

Need and the Long Tail of Health Inequalities

The model is organised around a specific definition of need: the ability of a person to benefit from a health intervention. This is distinct from demand (whether someone seeks a service) or supply (whether a service is available). A person needs sickle cell screening if they carry a genetic risk, regardless of whether they know about it or whether the screening programme exists locally. This definition, drawn from health needs assessment literature, is what connects the model's structure to the long tail concept.

In the model, need is the mechanism through which interventions affect outcomes. Each person either needs a given service or does not, and this is determined by their intersectional group membership — their ethnicity, deprivation, and the interaction between the two. When a person needs a service and receives it, the intervention's risk ratio reduces their mortality risk. When they need it but do not receive it, their increased risk of infant mortality remains unmitigated. When they do not need the service at all, it has no effect on them either way. Need is therefore the bridge between the intervention and the outcome: without need, there is no benefit and no harm from absence.

This matters because it shapes which interventions sit in the head of the distribution and which sit in the tail. An intervention's population-level impact depends not on its clinical potency alone but on how many people need it. Neonatal resuscitation has a powerful risk ratio, and because it is needed across all groups, it sits in the head — a blockbuster intervention with broad reach. A culturally specific screening programme may have a similarly strong effect per person, but because it is needed by a small subgroup, its population-level contribution is modest. It sits in the tail. Yet for the group that needs it, the benefit is substantial, and its absence represents a real and measurable increase in risk.

The long tail of health inequalities, as described by Kreuter and colleagues, is fundamentally a distribution of need across a population. The model operationalises this by allowing need to vary at the intersectional level — not just by broad ethnic category or deprivation band, but by their combination. This means the tail is populated by interventions that serve specific, often multiply disadvantaged groups whose needs would be invisible in an analysis that looked only at aggregate population benefit. The model makes these needs countable and their consequences visible, allowing users to trace directly from unmet need, through risk inflation, to preventable deaths — and to see how the cumulative weight of the tail compares to the head.
